A1-A2: Beginner Level

Comparative and Superlative Adjectives

Comparative adjectives are used to compare two objects, e.g., "bigger," "smarter." Superlative adjectives are used to express the highest level of quality, e.g., "the biggest," "the most beautiful."

Examples:

  • John is taller than Peter.
  • This is the most interesting book.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks in the sentences:

  1. My house is _____ (big) than yours.
  2. This is the _____ (good) movie I’ve ever seen.
Some / Any / No

Some, any, and no are used to express quantity or presence of something. "Some" is used in affirmative sentences, "any" in questions and negations, and "no" for complete negation.

Examples:

  • There is some water on the table.
  • Do you have any pencils?
  • There is no milk in the fridge.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. We don’t have _____ bread left. (We have some / any / no)
  2. Are there _____ cookies in the jar? (some / any / no)
Much / Many / A lot of / Few / Little

Much and many are used to express quantity, but "much" is used with uncountable nouns, while "many" is used with countable nouns. "A lot of" can be used with both, "few" means a small number, and "little" means a small amount.

Examples:

  • There isn’t much water in the glass.
  • How many apples do you want?
  • She has a lot of friends.
  • There are few people here.
  • We have little time left.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. How _____ sugar do you need for the cake? (much / many)
  2. We have _____ books on this subject. (few / a lot of)
Adjectives and Adverbs

Adjectives describe nouns, while adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Adverbs are often formed from adjectives with the "-ly" suffix.

Examples:

  • She is a fast runner. — adjective.
  • She runs quickly. — adverb.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. He speaks English very ______ (good). (Correct answer: well)
  2. She is a ______ (careful) driver. (Correct answer: careful)
Prepositions of Place and Time

Prepositions like in, on, at, by, with, for are used to indicate location or time.

Examples:

  • The book is on the table.
  • I will arrive at 6 o’clock.
  • We are meeting at the park.
  • She is sitting by the window.
  • This gift is for you.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. The keys are _____ the table. (on / at / in)
  2. We will meet _____ 5 PM. (at / on / in)
Question Words

Question words help us to ask questions: who, what, where, when, why, how.

Examples:

  • Who are you?
  • What is this?
  • Where are you going?
  • When does the train leave?
  • Why are you sad?
  • How do you know him?

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. _____ is your favorite color? (What / Where)
  2. _____ do you go to school? (How / Why)
Numbers

Cardinal numbers represent quantity (e.g., one, two, three), while ordinal numbers represent position (e.g., first, second, third).

Examples:

  • Cardinal numbers: one, two, three...
  • Ordinal numbers: first, second, third...

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. My birthday is on the ______ (3) of July. (Ordinal number)
  2. She has ______ (5) books. (Cardinal number)

A2-B1: Intermediate Level

All Basic Tenses

At this level, students learn the basic tenses: Present Simple, Present Continuous, Past Simple, Past Continuous, and Future Simple. Each tense is used for different types of actions in the present, past, and future.

Examples:

  • Present Simple: I play football every Sunday.
  • Present Continuous: I am playing football now.
  • Past Simple: I played football yesterday.
  • Past Continuous: I was playing football when it started raining.
  • Future Simple: I will play football tomorrow.

Exercise: Fill in the blanks:

  1. She ______ (go) to the park every day. (Present Simple)
  2. They ______ (study) English at the moment. (Present Continuous)
  3. I ______ (eat) dinner last night. (Past Simple)
Present Perfect vs. Past Simple

Present Perfect is used for actions that started in the past and have a connection to the present. Past Simple is used for completed actions in the past.

Examples:

  • Present Perfect: I have seen that movie.
  • Past Simple: I saw that movie yesterday.

Exercise: Choose the correct option:

  1. We ______ (have) dinner yet. (Present Perfect or Past Simple?)
  2. She ______ (go) to the cinema last weekend. (Present Perfect or Past Simple?)

B1-B1+: Upper Intermediate Level

Conditionals

Conditionals are used to talk about possible or hypothetical situations. There are four main types: Zero Conditional, First Conditional, Second Conditional, and Third Conditional.

Examples:

  • Zero Conditional: If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
  • First Conditional: If it rains, I will stay home.
  • Second Conditional: If I were rich, I would travel the world.
  • Third Conditional: If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.

Exercise: Fill in the blanks:

  1. If I ______ (study) harder, I would have passed the exam. (Third Conditional)
  2. If it ______ (rain), I will stay home. (First Conditional)
Passive Voice

In passive voice, the object of the sentence becomes the subject. It's commonly used when the action is more important than the person performing the action.

Examples:

  • Active: The teacher explains the lesson.
  • Passive: The lesson is explained by the teacher.

Exercise: Change to passive voice:

  1. They built the house last year. (Passive: The house ______ (build) last year.)
  2. She wrote the report. (Passive: The report ______ (write) by her.)
