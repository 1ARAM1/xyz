A1-A2: Beginner Level

Comparative and Superlative Adjectives

Comparative adjectives compare two things (e.g., bigger, smarter), while superlative adjectives show the highest degree of something (e.g., the biggest, the most beautiful).

Examples:

  • John is taller than Peter. (Comparative)
  • This is the most interesting book I've read. (Superlative)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. My house is _____ (big) than yours.
  2. This is the _____ (good) movie I’ve ever seen.
Some / Any / No

We use "some" for positive sentences, "any" for questions and negative sentences, and "no" for total negation.

Examples:

  • There is some milk in the fridge. (Positive)
  • Do you have any pens? (Question)
  • There are no apples left. (Negative)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. We don’t have _____ bread left.
  2. Are there _____ cookies in the jar?
Much / Many / A lot of / Few / Little

Use "much" with uncountable nouns and "many" with countable nouns. "A lot of" works for both. "Few" and "little" refer to small amounts, but "few" is for countable nouns and "little" is for uncountable nouns.

Examples:

  • There isn’t much water left.
  • How many books do you have?
  • She has a lot of friends.
  • There are few students in the classroom.
  • We have little time before the meeting starts.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. How _____ money do you need for the trip?
  2. We have _____ milk left in the fridge.
Adjectives and Adverbs

Adjectives describe nouns (things, people), and adverbs describe verbs (actions), adjectives, or other adverbs.

Examples:

  • He is a fast runner. (Adjective)
  • He runs quickly. (Adverb)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. She sings ______ (beautiful).
  2. He speaks English ______ (fluently).
Prepositions of Place and Time

Prepositions show where things are (place) and when something happens (time).

Examples:

  • The book is on the table. (Place)
  • The party starts at 8 PM. (Time)
  • We are meeting at the park. (Place)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. The keys are _____ the table. (on / in / at)
  2. We will meet _____ 5 PM. (at / on / in)
Question Words

Question words help us ask questions: who, what, where, when, why, and how.

Examples:

  • Who is that person?
  • What do you want to eat?
  • Where are you going?
  • When is your birthday?
  • Why are you upset?
  • How do you get to the station?

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. _____ is your favorite color? (What / Where)
  2. _____ do you go to the gym? (How / Why)
Numbers

Cardinal numbers express quantity (e.g., one, two), and ordinal numbers express position (e.g., first, second).

Examples:

  • Cardinal numbers: one, two, three...
  • Ordinal numbers: first, second, third...

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. My birthday is on the ______ (3) of July. (Ordinal number)
  2. She has ______ (5) pencils. (Cardinal number)

A2-B1: Intermediate Level

Present Simple vs. Present Continuous

We use Present Simple for routines and facts, and Present Continuous for actions happening right now.

Examples:

  • I go to the gym every morning. (Present Simple)
  • I am studying right now. (Present Continuous)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. She _____ (read) a book at the moment. (Present Continuous)
  2. We _____ (go) to the park every Sunday. (Present Simple)
Past Simple vs. Present Perfect

Past Simple is used for actions completed at a specific time in the past. Present Perfect is used for actions that happened at an unspecified time or have an effect on the present.

Examples:

  • I visited France last year. (Past Simple)
  • I have visited France. (Present Perfect)

Exercise:

Choose the correct option:

  1. We _____ (finish) our homework. (Present Perfect or Past Simple?)
  2. He _____ (see) that movie yesterday. (Present Perfect or Past Simple?)
First Conditional

We use the First Conditional to talk about real and possible situations in the future.

Example:

  • If it rains, I will stay at home.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. If she _____ (study) hard, she will pass the exam.

B1-B1+: Upper Intermediate Level

Second Conditional

We use the Second Conditional to talk about hypothetical or imaginary situations in the present or future.

Example:

  • If I were you, I would travel the world.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. If I _____ (be) rich, I would buy a big house.
Third Conditional

We use the Third Conditional to talk about hypothetical situations in the past—things that didn’t happen.

Example:

  • If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. If they _____ (leave) earlier, they would have caught the train.

Advanced Level

Modal Verbs

Modal verbs express necessity, possibility, permission, or ability. Common modal verbs include can, could, must, might, should, and would.

Examples:

  • You should study harder. (Advice)
  • She can swim very well. (Ability)
  • They might come tomorrow. (Possibility)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. You _____ (must) be more careful.
  2. He _____ (can) speak three languages.
Mixed Conditionals

Mixed Conditionals combine different time periods to show a connection between the past and present or future.

Example:

  • If I had studied harder, I would be a doctor now. (Past condition, present result)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. If she _____ (not miss) the bus, she _____ (arrive) on time.
